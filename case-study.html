01
Role A · 2021–2023
Operations & Project Lead
Building Organizational Infrastructure from Scratch
Background & Mandate

Upon entering the role, the unit existed on paper but was not functional. An initial drill conducted shortly after my arrival exposed structural failures: no defined workflows, personnel who didn't know their exact responsibilities, external interfaces that had broken down due to either a lack of suitable personnel or a failure to recognize the unit's value, and infrastructure that didn't match operational requirements. The mandate: build — people, processes, interfaces, infrastructure.

Entry State — What I Found
✗
Partial, unclear documentation — roles defined in name only, with no task breakdown or clear accountability
✗
Infrastructure mismatch — equipment and systems that didn't meet the requirements of different roles
✗
Broken external interfaces — some partners lacked capacity to coordinate; others didn't see the need
✗
No standardized workflows — every person operated on their own judgment with no defined process
⚠
Initial drill exposed all failures simultaneously — served as the trigger for a full redesign
Gap Matrix
Domain As-Is Gap To-Be
Documentation Partial and unclear. Roles defined by title only. No reliable knowledge source. Unit operates on tribal knowledge. Comprehensive Mission Book for every role — tasks, responsibilities, interfaces.
Role Clarity Personnel didn't know their exact tasks or accountability boundaries. Overlaps and blind spots in task ownership. Ambiguous accountability under pressure. Each role has a defined task list, clear scope, and a backup protocol for system failures.
Infrastructure Equipment didn't match role requirements. System failures halted all operations. Single point of failure with no fallback. Workstation specs characterized per role + defined manual failover procedure.
External Interfaces Interfaces existed on paper. In practice — inconsistently observed. External partners didn't understand the unit's role or value. Structured coordination protocols + standing working forum with each partner.
Timeline — What I Did
Mo. 1–2
🔍
Discovery & Gap Analysis
  • Initial drill that exposed structural failures
  • Mapping of existing and expected working interfaces
  • Gap analysis between requirements and existing infrastructure
Mo. 3–4
🔄
Process & Schedule Design
  • Redefined the unit's core mission
  • Designed shift roster with clear hierarchy and escalation path
  • Aligned schedule to realistic personnel capacity
Mo. 5
🛡
Infrastructure Specification & Risk Design
  • Characterized physical workstation specs per role
  • Designed manual failover procedure for system outages
  • Assigned a dedicated role to bridge connectivity failures
Mo. 6
📖
Documentation & Go-Live
  • Authored Mission Book covering all role positions
  • Built role-specific training materials for each station
  • Laid groundwork for future integration with the tech department
Outcome

By end of tenure, the unit had a defined organizational structure, documented processes, and formalized external interfaces — moving from an environment dependent on individual knowledge to one built on institutional process.

Note: Core systems remained in development and dependent on the tech department. The deliverable was the organizational and process foundation — not the technology layer.

02
Role B · 2021–2023
Command Center Commander
Concurrent with Role A
Process Institutionalization & Working Routine
Background

I initially served as an officer in the command center. Following a unit-level reorganization (at the parent unit level), the command center came under my direct authority. From the moment it reported to me, we overhauled how it operated — from a center that reported "when something interesting happens" to one with defined workflows, a systematic debrief culture, and regular drills.

Before & After
Before
✗
Vague mandate: "update when something interesting happens" — no criteria
✗
No response protocols — every officer operated on personal judgment
✗
No systematic debrief — no learning from past incidents
✗
No drills — personnel not prepared for high-pressure scenarios
After
✓
Defined which events to track and how — clear, documented criteria
✓
SOP for each incident type — uniform, fast response regardless of who's on shift
✓
After-action review after every incident — including successful ones
✓
Weekly drill routine for all shift personnel
Incident Response Flow
Step 1
👁
Incident Detection
  • Duty officer identifies incident from information sources
  • Baseline data collected: who, what, when
Step 2
⚡
Alert Officer & Confirm Data
  • Duty officer alerts on-call officer
  • Confirmed: incident nature, time, expected response timeline
Step 3
📢
Senior Leadership Update
  • Initial briefing call with collected data
  • Situation report + expected timeline
Step 4
🤝
External Contact
  • Reach out to responsible parties for additional intel
  • If unavailable — escalate to pre-defined alternate channel
Step 5
🔄
Ongoing Monitoring & Updates
  • Track developments — not on a fixed cycle, based on situation
  • Every material change passed to officer and leadership
Step 6
✅
Incident Closure
  • Closure only upon commander's explicit authorization
  • Summary documented for future after-action review
After-Action Review Framework

Every incident — positive or negative — went through a structured debrief:

1
What happened?
Description of the incident as it unfolded — timeline, actors involved, actual outcome.
2
What should have happened?
The standard / SOP that was supposed to be followed. What was the expected outcome.
3
Findings
Gaps between expected and actual — both positive and negative.
4
Root Cause Analysis
What caused the gaps? Human, process, or technology failure?
5
Lessons Learned
What can be extracted from this incident to improve future performance.
6
Recommendations
Concrete actions to execute — who does what, by when.
Outcome

The command center moved from intuition-based reporting to a shared language with defined processes. The team shifted from a reactive posture — responding to incidents — to one that identifies patterns before they escalate into crises.

Deputy Intelligence Officerr
Real-Time Execution · Crisis Management · 4–6 Direct Reports
Nature of the Role

Unlike the two previous roles focused on building, the Deputy Commander role was operational in nature — keeping the system running under pressure. Every delay, every failure, every logistical gap landed on the desk. One objective: ensure the commander never has to think about an operational detail.

Playbook — Emergency Activation at Zero Notice

Scenario: immediate order to transition from routine operations to full emergency — no preparation time.

1
Receive the Order
Receive the transition order. No time for questions — immediate execution begins.
2
Stand Up the War Room
Build the physical space from scratch — equipment, systems, communications. Each component checked by priority.
Priority order: communications → information systems → logistics
3
Systems Readiness Check
Verify all critical systems are operational. Identify failures and activate backup procedures as needed.
4
Resource Allocation
Prioritize personnel and resources by criticality. Ensure every station is staffed with qualified personnel.
5
Commander Briefing
Deliver a full situation report — what's working, what isn't, what's being handled. The commander focuses on mission, not operations.
Guiding Principle

The Deputy's job is not to build — it's to keep what was built working when things break. Every decision is measured by one question: can the commander stay focused on the mission?
